Artist statement:

For Synthesis 2, I set out to create a data visualization comparing the prices of two grocery stores, Aldi and Costco. Prices were sourced from a mixture of in-store observation, online archives of weekly advertisements, and in Costco's case, an existing spreadsheet put together by Heather Clarke of queenbeecoupons.com.

I chose these two stores because they both carry a local reputation for low prices. Costco's reputation in particular is that the prices are so low, they justify a $55 annual membership to be able to even shop there. They also both carry a large enough variety of products that products from across many categories could be selected for comparison. 74 products were analyzed in total.

The prices are given in the format of a bar chart with two adjacent bars for each product allowing for quick visual comparison. Hovering over each bar allows the user to see product name and exact price value. Note that while the price for each item is given in "per units", the unit itself differs from product to product - for example, milk's price is given per gallon, while chicken's price is given per pound. Other products are measured in price per individual unit (mostly household products: batteries, trash bags, etc.)

I originally wanted this visualization to be accompanied by a set of buttons that would allow the user to view products in a specific category (grocery, snacks, dairy, etc.) to allow for more indepth comparison and/or to examine trends within a category. Because of this, I used Bokeh to build the master graph shown above, and planned to include a radio button group widget with interactivity programmed. However, after many attempts I was sadly unable to figure out how to make it work (specifically, I had issues with importing my data into a CustomJS callback). The remnants of my attempts to do so can be seen in my Jupyter Notebook (included).

I chose this topic because as someone that grocery shops on a tight budget, I'm constantly making value comparisons to determine the best way to stretch a dollar, and no doubt many other students are in the same boat. While many online comparisons already exist between grocery stores, they tend to be in the format of spreadsheets or price lists. These could let a potential customer determine the difference between a few items, but they are generally not good at communicating overall trends.

The trend between these two stores is that their prices are relatively close, with only a few products swinging heavily in favor of one store or the other. Factors such as travel time, method of transportation (someone on the bus may have a hard time transporting bulk purchases from Costco), brand/product availability, and of course the aforementioned $55 / year membership mean that different people will determine different courses of action from this data. However, it is a useful visualization for all people looking to effectively manage their budget.
